Artificial intelligence, explained without the jargon

Four ideas, each one built on the last — AI, machine learning, deep learning, and generative AI — explained in plain language, with something to click, drag, or test at every step.

Reading progress · 0% — a little like a generative model: the more of this page it's "read," the more confident its next guess.

You'll see these four ideas over and over: artificial intelligence is the broad goal, machine learning is one way to get there, deep learning is a particular style of machine learning, and generative AI is a particular thing you can build with deep learning. Each section below explains one idea, gives you a real analogy, shows the actual (simple) math behind it, and lets you try it yourself.

01 / Foundation

Artificial intelligence: a destination, not one specific method

"Artificial intelligence" doesn't name one technique — it names a goal: getting a computer to do something that normally needs human judgment, understanding, or skill. There are two very different ways to reach that goal. You can write out every rule yourself, by hand. Or you can show the computer a lot of examples and let it work out the rules on its own. Only the second one is machine learning — but both count as artificial intelligence.

Analogy

Think of two ways to make a good cook. You could hand someone a recipe card with exact steps — that's a hand-written rule system. Or you could have them taste a thousand dishes made by others until they develop their own sense of what works — that's learning from examples. Both can produce a good meal. Only one of them "learned" anything.

In one line

$$\text{AI} \;=\; \{\text{hand-written rule systems}\} \;\cup\; \{\text{systems that learn from data}\}$$

AI = "hand-written rule systems" combined with "systems that learn from data"

∪combined with / the union of both groups
{ }a set — a group of things sharing a property

02 / Approach

Machine learning: showing, not telling

Machine learning means training an algorithm on a pile of labeled examples so it can make a good guess about something new it hasn't seen — without you writing an explicit rule for every possible case. You supply examples and their correct answers; the computer works out what the pattern is.

Analogy

It's how most people learn to sort laundry as a kid — not from a rulebook, but from watching a parent do it hundreds of times. Nobody hands you a formal definition of "delicate fabric." You just absorb the pattern from enough labeled examples ("this goes in cold water," "this one shrinks").

In one line

$$\text{score}(e) = \sum_{i=1}^{3} \mathbb{1}[\,f_i = e_i\,]$$

score(example) = how many of the 3 features match the new item — then guess the label of whichever past example scores highest

eone past labeled example
fa feature of the new, unlabeled item
𝟙[…]1 if the condition is true, otherwise 0
scorehow well example e matches the new item

03 / Depth

Deep learning: when one line isn't enough

A "neural network" is just a stack of tiny decision-makers ("neurons"), each one adding up its inputs, weighting them, and producing a yes/no-ish output. One neuron alone can only draw a straight boundary through your data. Combine several into a layer — and stack multiple layers on top of each other — and the network as a whole can bend that boundary into shapes a single neuron never could. That's the practical payoff of "deep": more neurons and more layers generally mean the network can fit more complex, curved patterns, and accuracy tends to climb as it grows — which is exactly why deep learning is so good at messy, real-world patterns like photos and speech.

Analogy

One bouncer checking a single thing (ID showing 21+) can only ever make one kind of decision. A whole team of bouncers, each checking something different — ID, dress code, guest list — and combining their answers, can enforce a far more nuanced door policy than any one of them alone. Deep learning is many simple checks, combined.

In one line

$$\hat y = \mathbb{1}\big[\,w_1x_1 + w_2x_2 + b \ge 0\,\big]$$

ŷ = 1 if (w₁·x₁ + w₂·x₂ + b) ≥ 0, otherwise 0 — one neuron's whole job

x₁, x₂the inputs — the features being weighed
w₁, w₂weights — how much each input matters
bbias — shifts the decision boundary
ŷthe neuron's predicted class

04 / Output

Generative AI: autocomplete, scaled way up

Generative AI is trained to predict what comes next — the next word, the next pixel, the next sound — based on patterns in huge amounts of existing content. Generate one small piece, add it to what you have, predict the next piece, repeat. Do that enough times and you get a brand new sentence, image, or song that was never directly copied from anywhere, just built to fit the patterns the model learned.

Analogy

A musician who's listened to thousands of songs can improvise something new that still "sounds right" — the right notes, the right rhythm — without playing any single song note-for-note. They've absorbed the patterns of the genre, not memorized one song. That's generative AI, at a much larger scale.

In one line

$$P(w_{t+1}=n \mid w_t=p) \;=\; \dfrac{\text{count}(p \rightarrow n)}{\sum_k \text{count}(p \rightarrow k)}$$

P(next word = n | current word = p) = how often n followed p, divided by how often anything followed p

wtthe current word
wt+1the word being predicted
p, na specific "previous" / "next" word
count(p→n)how many times n followed p in training text

05 / Synthesis

Nested, chained, or timed — pick the lens that answers your question

These four ideas get drawn as nested circles all the time, which makes it easy to mix up two different things: what's built using what, and what was invented when. They usually line up — you generally need the broader field to exist before someone can specialize into the narrower one — but they're answering different questions. Toggle between the three views below.

One nuance the diagrams above simplify: generative techniques don't strictly require deep learning — the word-predictor demo above is technically "generative" and used no neural network at all. What's true is narrower: today's most powerful generative AI happens to be built on deep learning, which is why the "built using" chain holds for the systems you're most likely to actually use.

Summary

The four ideas, compressed

AIThe goal: computers doing things that normally need human judgment.
MLOne approach: learn the rules from labeled examples instead of hand-coding them.
DLA style of ML: stack many simple neurons to capture complex patterns.
GenAIA goal for DL: predict the next piece, over and over, to create something new.

Common pitfalls

  • The nested-circles diagram means "is built using / is a kind of" — not "arrived in this order," even though the two usually line up.
  • Generative AI doesn't strictly require deep learning — simple statistical models can generate too. Today's most capable generative systems just happen to be deep-learning-based.
  • A confident-sounding answer isn't the same as a correct one. Generative AI predicts what's statistically likely, not what's verified true — which is the root of "hallucination."
